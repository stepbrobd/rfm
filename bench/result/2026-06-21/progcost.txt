Per-packet eBPF datapath cost (kernel.bpf_stats_enabled=1), real i40e NIC,
offered ~2.1 Mpps of 64B over 1000 flows, sample rate N=100. Produced by
bench/workload/progcost.nu. run_cnt/run_time_ns summed over the agent's
sched_cls (TC/TCX) programs over a 6 s window; rx_pkts from the NIC counter.

  agent      progs  runs_per_rx  avg_ns_in_prog   head-to-head sys_cores
  -----      -----  -----------  --------------   ----------------------
  none         -        -             -           6.01 (NIC RX softirq only)
  rfm          2      1.00          118           6.61  (+0.60, 0 ring drops)
  hsflowd      2      1.00           98          21.17  (+15.2, epcap/TCX)
  netobserv  >=2      1.00           74          23.05  (+17.0, Sampling:100)
  softflowd    -        -             -          21.67  (+15.7, libpcap)
  pmacctd      -        -             -          27.88  (+21.9, libpcap)

Interpretation (the load-bearing fact for the paper): every agent's TC/TCX
program executes on every packet (runs_per_rx = 1.00) -- sampling does NOT skip
packets at the datapath. netobserv ran with Sampling:100 confirmed
(TCAttachMode:tcx, CacheMaxFlows:5000), yet its program still runs per packet
because its per-CPU hash AGGREGATES every packet and sampling only thins the
EXPORT. rfm's program also runs per packet (its eBPF is in fact the most
expensive per run, 118 ns, because it updates the always-on exact per-CPU
counters), but it holds NO per-flow kernel state and gates the sample FIRST: 99%
of packets stop after the counter bump and never reach userspace, so the total
system cost is +0.60 cores vs +15..+22 for the agents that keep per-flow state
(netobserv) or copy every packet to userspace (libpcap). The cost gap is about
where flow state lives, not about whether the datapath touches every packet.
